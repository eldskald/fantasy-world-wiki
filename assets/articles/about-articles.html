About Articles

This article is here to explain some basic functionalities about articles.

Article file names and titles

When you upload your articles, to update your page with them we will scan them for their titles. The title is assumed to be whatever is inside the h1 header tag. This title will be used on the articles index page. You can access it to see all articles of your page listed, and every entry has the text inside its h1 tag. This means that having one h1 header inside every article is obligatory. It doesn't need to be the first tag, it can be anywhere on the article and you can even have more than one if you want to, but I think it would look ugly and confusing. You can see this article for an example of an article whose first tag isn't an h1 tag.

Lastly, the filename you choose is also important. It will be the URL for your article and the name you'll to link to it. You might have noticed how navigating through maps and articles changes the URL. Whatever is after the article= part on your URL is the article filename for the article you are currently reading, without the .html extension of course. It is also the name you will use on links, see next session.

About links

You might have noticed that links use an a tag. It stands for anchor. If you want to link to an article, give it a toarticle attribute with the filename of the article you want to link to and it should work. Similarly for maps, a tomap attribute with the filename of the map you're trying to link to should work. You can link to both an article and a map too. Lastly, you can also put a tofragment attribute to put a fragment on the link, that is, put it with the id of an element on your document to have it scroll to it. Preferably, you use tofragment together with toarticle, where tofragment has the id of an element in the document of the linked article in toarticle. For more information about id's, see the about the table of contents section.

If you haven't found links like this already, here is a link to this same article so clicking it should do nothing, and here is a link to map two. You can put links to external websites as well, like this link to the project repository. You can just copy this syntax and replace the URL on the href attribute for the URL you want to link to.

About the table of contents

You might have noticed every article has a table of contents at the beginning. You have to put it there, it's the table-of-contents tag that's on every article's source file. You can just copy that line and put it wherever you want the table of contents to be in.

The way it works is that it will scan your entire article for h2, h3 and h4 tags and list them, the text inside the tags is what will be listed in the table of contents. If you click any link in it, it will scroll directly to this section in the article. Also, look at what happens to your URL. You will see a #section-name added to the end of it. This text is the id of the tag. You don't need an id on every header, but only those with one can be linked to and as the name suggests, every id must be unique. Any header of types h2, h3 and h4 without ids will that get scanned by the table of contents will be given a number as an id so its links can scroll to it.

You can completely ignore and give no ids to your headers, everything will still work as normal. I just put ids in all of them so all of my URLs look prettier and human-readable. Otherwise, the URLs will have numbers in them instead of whatever I wrote for the id. You click around these links and experiment, don't be afraid to mess around.

About HTML

Lastly, you might have noticed that articles are written in HTML. If you don't know what that is, that's fine. Some of you might recognize some of this syntax from using old forums, and that's exactly what it is. Everything is written inside these <tag-name>contents</tag-name> tags, and tags can contain tags as well. The p tag stands for paragraph while the h tags stands for headers, the lower the number the more important the header, so h1 header is the biggest header while the h6 header is the lowest.

Most of your articles will be just paragraphs and header tags, with the ocasional img tag for images (more on images here) and other tags for tables and notes (see here for tables and here for notes), as well as ul or ol for lists (more about lists here).

In case none of these are what you're looking for, I'd recommend looking into this page to learn more about HTML.
